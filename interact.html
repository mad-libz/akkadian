Timeline of the Akkadian Empire

2334 BCE

Sargon of Akkad establishes the Akkadian Empire, unifying the city-states of Mesopotamia under his rule.

2310 BCE

Sargon conducts military campaigns to expand the empire's borders, asserting dominance over neighboring regions.

2300 BCE

Sargon's conquests extend Akkadian influence to the regions of Sumer and Elam, solidifying the empire's control over southern Mesopotamia.

2270 BCE

Naram-Sin, grandson of Sargon, continues the expansion of the Akkadian Empire, reaching its zenith in territorial extent.

2250 BCE

Economic strain and environmental factors contribute to internal unrest and rebellions within the empire.

2220 BCE

The Gutians, a tribal people from the Zagros Mountains, invade Mesopotamia, challenging Akkadian authority.

2200 BCE

Gutian incursions and internal strife weaken the Akkadian Empire, leading to the fragmentation of central control.

2180 BCE

Gutian forces capture the city of Akkad, marking the symbolic end of Akkadian dominance in Mesopotamia.

2150 BCE

The Akkadian Empire collapses, giving rise to a period of regional fragmentation and the resurgence of independent city-states.

2120 BCE

City-states such as Ur and Lagash emerge as centers of power in post-Akkadian Mesopotamia.

2100 BCE

Sumerian city-states regain autonomy, ending the era of Akkadian hegemony in Mesopotamia.

2083 BCE

The Third Dynasty of Ur briefly reunites much of Mesopotamia under Sumerian rule, marking a new phase in the region's history.

2050 BCE

The Amorites migrate into Mesopotamia, establishing independent kingdoms and further reshaping the political landscape.

2000 BCE

Babylon rises to prominence as a major political and cultural center in southern Mesopotamia, eventually eclipsing the former Akkadian capital of Akkad.

1900 BCE

The rise of the Babylonian Empire under Hammurabi marks the beginning of a new era in Mesopotamian history, with Babylonian rule supplanting Akkadian influence.

End of timeline
